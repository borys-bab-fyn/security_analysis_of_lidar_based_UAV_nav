2. KEY FINDINGS — REFLECTIVE SURFACE TESTS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  Angle   | Actual Dist | Mean Sensor | Mean Error | Error %  | Std Dev | Range
  --------|-------------|-------------|------------|----------|---------|----------
    0°    |  22.6 cm    |  27.53 cm   | +4.93 cm   | +21.8%   |  4.44   | 18–35
   45°    |  29.0 cm    |  34.97 cm   | +5.97 cm   | +20.6%   |  1.77   | 33–39

  • The LiDAR sensor consistently OVERESTIMATES distance when measuring
    reflective surfaces, by roughly +5–6 cm (~21%) at both tested angles.
  • At 0° incidence the readings are highly variable (SD = 4.44 cm, range
    spanning 17 cm), indicating the specular reflection sends the beam on
    unpredictable paths.
  • At 45° incidence the readings are more tightly clustered (SD = 1.77 cm)
    but the positive bias is even larger (+5.97 cm). The angled surface
    produces a more consistent — but still erroneous — return.
  • No data was recorded for 15°, 30°, or 60° angles, so the full angular
    dependence cannot be characterised from this dataset.


3. KEY FINDINGS — TRANSPARENT SURFACE TESTS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  Actual Dist   | Mean Sensor | Mean Error | Error %  | Std Dev | Range
  --------------|-------------|------------|----------|---------|----------
  30.1 ± 0.1 cm|  36.63 cm   | +6.53 cm   | +21.7%   |  1.47   | 34–38

  • The sensor again OVERESTIMATES distance, now by +6.53 cm (+21.7%).
  • The overestimation is the largest of all three test conditions, consistent
    with the laser partially passing through the transparent material and
    reflecting off a surface behind or within it.
  • Variability is moderate (SD = 1.47 cm), similar to the 45° reflective
    case, suggesting that the transparent surface produces a relatively
    stable — but inaccurate — return signal.
  • Every single reading (34–38 cm) exceeds the true distance (30.1 cm);
    not a single measurement fell at or below the actual value.


4. IMPLICATIONS FOR LiDAR VULNERABILITY RESEARCH
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
These results demonstrate that both reflective and transparent surfaces
introduce systematic positive distance bias (~20–22%) in LiDAR
measurements, with reflective surfaces at normal incidence also causing
high measurement variance. This means an adversary could exploit common
materials — mirrors, glass, polished metal — to make objects appear farther
away than they actually are, potentially causing LiDAR-dependent systems
(e.g., autonomous vehicles, security perimeters) to misjudge obstacle
proximity. The consistency of the bias across all test conditions suggests
that simple material-based spoofing attacks are feasible without
sophisticated electronic equipment.
